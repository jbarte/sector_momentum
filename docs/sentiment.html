How is the sentiment score calculated?

Source — FinBERT news sentiment

The headline sentiment score is built from recent financial news, not search attention. For each of the 11 GICS sectors we fetch the latest 24 hours of English-language headlines from the GDELT global news index (up to 250 per sector, matched via sector-specific topic codes). Each headline is scored by ProsusAI/finbert, a finance-tuned BERT model that classifies text as positive, negative or neutral with a confidence score. The per-sector mean signed polarity (positive = +score, negative = −score, neutral = 0) is then z-scored across the 11 sectors: positive = more bullish news vs peers, negative = more bearish. Both US and EU rows for the same GICS sector share the same score — financial news sentiment is global. If FinBERT or GDELT is unavailable, the score falls back to the Google Trends slope z-score.

Google Trends derived signals

The table also shows search-attention signals from Google Trends (information-only — they do not affect the ranking). For each sector we pull ~13 weeks of worldwide search interest for its primary keyword (e.g. Technology → semiconductor, Energy → oil), queried per region (US in US search, EU averaged across Germany, France and the UK) and normalized against a neutral anchor term.

Themes

The Themes cohort uses Google Trends only (no FinBERT). Themes map to real search phrases (uranium, defense stocks, robotics) rather than ETF tickers. Theme interest is pulled worldwide (themes are global) and z-scored across the theme cohort. Switch cohorts with the toggle above.

Table columns

The sector table shows both FinBERT and Trends signals (all information-only except Polarity, which feeds the ranking via the blend toggle):

  • Momentum (Trends) — trend/slope of search interest.
  • Acceleration (Trends) — recent-half slope minus earlier-half; positive = attention picking up speed.
  • Range position (Trends) — latest reading within its 13-week min–max (0% = low, 100% = high).
  • Spike (Trends) — z-score of the latest point vs trailing weeks; a breakout in interest.
  • Volatility (Trends) — how choppy week-to-week interest has been.
  • Attention (Trends) — relative search interest compared head-to-head against all other sectors. Higher = more attention.
  • Seasonal (Trends) — last 13 weeks vs prior 39-week baseline. Above 1.0 = exceeding historical norm.
  • Polarity (FinBERT) — mean signed sentiment from recent news headlines. Green = bullish, red = bearish.
  • Articles (FinBERT) — number of headlines scored.
  • Pos% (FinBERT) — fraction classified as positive.
  • Neg% (FinBERT) — fraction classified as negative.
  • Rising Queries (Trends) — emerging search terms. Click a row with ▶ to expand.

It does not drive the ranking by default

The leaderboard composite is built from price/momentum signals only. Sentiment changes the ranking only when you enable the Sentiment toggle, at the weight you choose. Treat it as a confirmer, not a driver — search interest is noisy and can lag price.

Bottom-right quadrant (data ahead of sentiment) is the early-rotation zone. Hollow points = no sentiment data this scan (source unavailable or EU-only). Sentiment is a confirming signal — noisy, gameable, retail-biased. This is analytical tooling, not investment advice.
